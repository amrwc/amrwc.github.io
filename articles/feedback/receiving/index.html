Receiving Feedback

Getting feedback can be stressful

If one had a negative experience with receiving feedback in the past, the prospect of the process can trigger anxiety. It’s an evolutionary response to things humans perceive as threats.1

If that happens to you, try your best to relax – remaining stressed won’t help you analyse and reflect on the feedback. It may trigger a defensive response and block out any reasonable thought process that could help us get better at what you do.

Understand their perspective

If the feedback is vague, or seems off, try to understand the perspective of the person giving the feedback. Don’t dismiss feedback just because you disagree or you cannot recall the situation.

It helps to have examples of the given actions and/or behaviours that displayed what the person means. Perhaps you said something in a certain way, or have made a decision that affected others and it seemed rushed. Remember that, on average, we perceive ourselves in a more positive light than others do.

The circumstances matter – maybe you had a bad day when the given behaviour occurred. However, we must remember that a single instance of a behaviour may shape how others see us in the future. It’s the first impression effect.

It doesn’t always need to be in writing

A conversation may be a more accessible way for someone to share feedback. Maybe they’re not confident in their writing skills, or they’re unable to articulate what they feel in a good way in text. Or maybe they struggle to find the time or to focus on this and too much time passes before they come back with the feedback.

Offer to chat before they sit down to write something. It can be helpful to hear them out and maybe clarify some misunderstandings from the past before they’re written down and submitted.

This invitation to a conversation may happen before you submit the written feedback. Or you can share the draft request with them and ask for their opinion in advance. Perhaps they have some good tips on the request itself.

Don’t dissect the feedback too much

There’s a thin line between clearing up some (constructive) criticism that may be based in a misunderstanding and appearing defensive and ungrateful for the feedback. Remember that a person may feel vulnerable sharing their honest thoughts about you in their feedback. They may become defensive if they sense that you disagree with their feedback.
