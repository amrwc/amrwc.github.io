Feedback-Driven Culture

Part of the Feedback series.

Feedback is an opinion, grounded in observations and experiences, which allows us to know what impression we make on others.1

It is a humbling experience to receive and reflect on feedback. It may be positive – reaffirm our talents and hard work – or it can point out our imperfections, things we need to continue working on – constructive criticism. Someone else’s thoughts on the way we do things – if used correctly – can help us grow both professionally and in everyday life. A healthy dose of growth mindset is required to improve oneself upon receiving feedback.2

It’s worth going through a round of feedback every now and then to receive actionable advice on how to get better at what we do. It doesn’t just help us on our career trajectory, it helps the whole team perform better.

There are 3 main types of feedback: appreciation, coaching, and evaluation.3 The advice in these articles mostly focuses on the evaluation and coaching feedback types, less so on the appreciation, which is nonetheless important. The article combines some practical tips around requesting, receiving, and giving feedback in a professional setting. It is about conscious, explicitly requested feedback, not about unsolicited advice, ad-hoc suggestions, and tips given in passing.

Continuous self-improvement

If there’s a specific part of your personal or professional growth that you’d like to improve, check with others how you’re doing. By reflecting on feedback and taking action, you grow as a professional and as a person. The team grows with you as a whole. The received feedback shapes you into the colleague your teammates need you to be.

It’s possible that there’s something in your day to day work you should improve but haven’t realised it yet. That something may had been noticed by your colleague and they could offer advice on how to change it for the better, and that can become your growth focus. Observations of one person on the team may be shared by others and so their feedback may improve the collaboration not just with them, but with the rest of the team too.

Continuous performance management

Feedback, if it’s honest and shared often, becomes a tool for continuous performance management.4 If a teammate does not perform as expected or displays behaviours that negatively impact the team, letting them know soon after noticing gives them a chance to act on it early. Otherwise, the person may not realise that they negatively affect others, and if it goes on for too long, it might be too late to repair the relationships and trust.

A team can be self-sufficient in this way without the manager’s intervention – the contributors who collaborate on a daily basis know more about each other’s efforts than the manager. By extension, when a manager finds out about an issue with a contributor, it normally happens some time after the other contributors have noticed. If a team shares feedback often, such an issue may already be in the process of resolving by the time the manager realises.

A few rounds of feedback become a fantastic part of evidence of someone’s performance. From the manager’s point of view, such data – if visible to them – become an invaluable resource. Feedback among direct reports shows the collaboration trends of the team, what everyone expects from each other, and how everyone performs in their role.

Self-reinforcing positive loop

At the team level, you lead by example when you ask for feedback outside the mandatory performance review cycle. If the team is sceptical about asking for feedback more often than they’re used to, your actions may inspire them to do the same. Connect with the people around you – be honest, communicate openly, build trust. Listen to people and understand their perspectives and expectations to build the foundation upon which the feedback culture will grow.5 Always offer to provide feedback in return. This way they will be sure they can count on you repaying them with the same effort they put for you. Encourage the practice — whenever you request feedback, announce to the team that some or all will receive a request soon.

At an individual level, you improve your skills and behaviours, and you validate the efforts through the feedback. Over time, after a few iterations of feedback between you and your colleagues and some encouragement for others to do the same, chances are that people around you will give this practice a shot.
