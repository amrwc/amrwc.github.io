Giving Feedback

Part of the Feedback series.

Feedback must aid someone’s professional or personal development, not be used as a weapon. For it to be helpful, it must be specific and as objective as possible – give examples of situations rather than your feelings.

3 main types of feedback: appreciation, coaching, and evaluation

There are 3 main types of feedback and they should be used during moments appropriate to each: appreciation, coaching, and evaluation.1 For example, we shouldn’t attempt to evaluate someone immediately after they’ve given their best, we should praise them instead to encourage them to continue growing. Or when someone’s in trouble and in need of advice, praising them won’t help them – they need coaching.

Appreciation encourages further growth, aids the positive feedback loop. Coaching helps people grow. Evaluation, ideally as free from bias as possible, paints the picture of current performance.

With the evaluation results, one can devise a coaching plan. Appreciation motivates growing further and helps seek and accept further coaching (positive feedback loop).

Find a connection

It’s useful to understand a person’s motivations when offering them feedback. What you care about in a job may not be the same as the other person’s aspirations. For a coaching feedback to be effective, try to align it with their goals.

When you have to provide a critical evaluation feedback, it may help to know that a major event happened in the person’s personal life that could have affected their performance. We must be empathetic to the context beyond our workplace.

Some people may not enjoy being praised in public. It good to know that in advance, before we give them a shout-out in public for how good they’ve been doing.

Another reason to build a relationship with the other person is that it plays a key role in how they interpret our feedback and whether they will act on it. We accept some people’s criticism better than from others. If they don’t trust our judgement, they may reject our feedback.2

Be as objective as possible

Instead of letting your emotions take over, describe the factual the specific action or behaviour that took place and the context behind it. Don’t begin a piece of feedback with how you felt someone did; describe the context of the situation – for example, ‘during project X I have seen you do Y which led to Z’. Try to detach the objective description of the situation from your emotional response to it.

Use the Situation, Behaviour, Impact framework.3 Situation is the context around the action or behaviour – be specific about the context because it helps the other person understand what you mean and remember the details. Behaviour – describe the observable behaviour without assuming what the person felt or thought. Impact is either your reaction to the behaviour, or some facts that you may be aware of that stemmed from the situation.

With the situation already described to the person and making sure they understand your perspective, you can provide actionable advice. Perhaps there’s a tip or two you can share that could help the person improve their skills.

Give a heads-up before giving unsolicited advice

People can get defensive with unsolicited advice. If they haven’t explicitly requested feedback from you and you still want to offer them a word of advice, let them know in advance that you want to share some words of advice about some specific situation. For example, ask someone: ‘Could we discuss meeting X that you facilitated? I have some thoughts I’d like to share with you.’ This way you give someone a fair warning that you want to give feedback so that they can mentally prepare for it and tell you when the right time would be to have the conversation.
