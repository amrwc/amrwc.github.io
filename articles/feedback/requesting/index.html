Requesting Feedback

If you want to improve, you must request feedback yourself, not wait for it to come.1 Feedback uncovers our blind spots – we don’t see ourselves the same way others do.2

Request feedback soon after some conclusion

Right after a project milestone has been finished, a presentation has been given, or a meeting has ended, the details are fresh in the participants’ memories. Especially when your part was significant, it is well worth spending some time coming up with a feedback request right away.

Your colleagues may or may not immediately have thoughts specific to your contribution, unless you explicitly asked to lead the given meeting or initiative. Ask for feedback as soon as possible, and give them an extra reason to reflect on your involvement.

You’ve led an initiative for a while already and it starts to take shape? Ask how you’ve been doing, how your ideas and experience propelled the team forward, and what you could’ve done better. You’ve given a series of mentoring sessions or onboarded someone to the team? Ask them how this experience impacted their work. Perhaps you’re the one being onboarded to the team. How have you been doing thus far; what positive impact were you able to have on the team already?

Don’t wait until the performance review cycle to ask for feedback – other people’s decaying recollection of your efforts works against you. Moreover, people tend to remember the negatives for longer than the positives, be strategic about when you request feedback.

Be specific

Asking broad questions may lead to analysis paralysis in a person when faced with such a feedback request. For instance, asking ‘how am I doing at the moment?’ is less effective than asking ‘how has my effort towards X affected Y?’.

Focused feedback questions help the other person give you concrete actionable advice that you require.3 It’s much more effective to gather feedback on things that you find most important and act on it than to receive broad opinion on things you may not find as impactful.

Ask multiple people

Having multiple perspectives helps us eliminate biases that may transpire. Each peer’s feedback will contain some valuable nugget of information we can act upon.

Moreover, each person’s experience with you may be very different, and so will lead to different feedback to consider.

Ask yourself: what feedback would they possibly give you?

Coming up with the right feedback request questions may be tricky. Try working on them backwards; think what would be an ideal feedback response from the given person, and formulate questions leading them in that direction.

You should have a rough idea of what the person’s perception of you might be. What good things might they have to say about you? Did you do something great together that will help their team work more efficiently? Ask them about it:

How did our collaboration impact your team’s ways of working?

Keep in mind that if you’ve had a negative interaction, it may – of course – result in not-so-positive feedback.

Make it easy to give you feedback

Giving feedback may be daunting. It may trigger negative emotions corresponding to someone’s past experience of filling out vague forms encompassing long time periods trying to remember anything about a colleague’s work. Or someone may be unsure how the other person would respond to the feedback they would be compelled to provide.

To receive feedback without having to nag people for it, we must make giving feedback accessible. Key part of it is to ask for feedback early.

Ask ‘What’ and ‘How’ questions4 to avoid close-ended ‘Yes’ and ‘No’ answers. Go with ‘What do you think of my contributions during the project XYZ?’ rather than ‘Have I done well during project XYZ?’.

Only ask 2-4 questions – you don’t want them to have to write an essay to answer all your questions.

Ask questions tailored to the person and your past collaboration with them. Probe for their opinion on the things you think you’ve done well. Go with ‘How did my solution X help the project succeed?’ instead of ‘What did I do well?’. Frame your questions in context – the question is the canvas, their answer paints the picture.

Leave room for a free-form answer. Use the last question for something along the lines of ‘What do you think I should work on to continue growing as an XYZ?’. It opens the door for any leftover thoughts the person might have about you after thinking of the previous questions.

Give the person a chance to give you feedback in more than one form. Perhaps they prefer to go for a walk outside the office, or have a video call, or maybe a chat over text. Having an informal feedback session may help to clear up some possible misunderstandings.

Ask for examples

Ask for examples of situations when requesting feedback to underline the person’s perspective. They will help you understand their perspective.

It can be as simple as an extra line at the end of the feedback request:

Please add examples and explanations where applicable.

If the feedback request is narrowed down to some event or milestone, it will be easier for the person to recall the details.

Include a question about your relationship with the person

Imagine someone reading the feedback you’ve received. They may have only recently joined the company and may not know the people who gave you the feedback. They could reach out to them to ask for their perspective but they may not remember the circumstances around the feedback. Worse yet, the people who gave you the feedback may have left the company already.

It’s good to make the relationship between the people involved in the feedback round obvious and explicit. Were you a direct report of the person who gave you the feedback or were they reporting to you? Maybe they’re from a different department and you wanted to hear their thoughts on your cross-departmental collaboration. Have you worked together directly on a project, or did you just handle the alignment of multiple initiatives? How often have you collaborated?

This question can be the first one in your feedback request. It can also serve to warm them up with some easy-to-answer question, and open their mind to consider how your collaboration looked like overall.

Review the request draft with someone

You could run the feedback request by your manager. They may have some great advice on the questions you could ask others. Also, they will probably have a question or two in mind about you that they would like to have answered by your peers. Maybe they were already considering asking the others something about you but you came to them with this feedback request at the right time.

If you have a mentor, consider having them read the feedback request for similar reasons. Maye they will be able to share some good tips, or advise on the best people to request the feedback from.

Single textbox

NOTE: This is advice quite situational and sometimes separating questions may be the better choice. Some software, such as Workday, may treat each feedback textbox as a separate feedback, removing the context and making it awkward to read the entire submission at once. Know your medium.

Consider composing the questions in such a way that could be answered together. Imagine the request to be an email – a single textbox filled with your questions. In response, they also are presented with the same empty box that they must fill, guided by your questions.

Try not to split the contexts of each question into separate textboxes. Remember how it feels to stare at a blank page when tasked with writing a document or a paper? We can spare the responder from this feeling for subsequent questions if we give them only one place to answer them. Having answered a question or two already, they will be able to keep the flow going, and the answers may blend with one another.

Be careful what you wish for

NOTE: Maybe skip this section?

At the risk of providing unethical advice: be careful about requesting feedback.

If the feedback is shared with your manager, you must remember that it will reflect on you. Therefore, if you know someone may have bad or mixed feedback to share about you, think twice about the possible consequences. If you’re working towards a promotion, before asking such person for feedback, try to improve upon what you suspect they might have noticed in your work. Perhaps bring your efforts to their attention somehow so that they have something good to say about it.

Remember about framing the feedback request in some context. Try to frame the request as an honest learning opportunity to do better next time. It shows humility and willingness to improve — valuable traits in a collaborative environment.
